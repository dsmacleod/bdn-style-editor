**How to write headlines at the BDN**

Headline writing for the internet is an artform. The right headline can make a story go viral, and the wrong one can doom it. It’s the most valuable tool that we have to ensure that a good story gets read by as many people as possible.

**So what does a good headline entail? Here are some guidelines:**

1. A good headline compels you to read the story.  
2. It’s written in a conversational style.   
3. It clearly describes in one sentence the most interesting and essential part of the story.   
4. It answers at least two of the five W’s: Who, What, When, Where, Why.  
5. It elicits an emotional response.  
6. It makes sense with the featured image.

**First, some hard rules:**

1. Use the strongest verb possible.   
2. Don’t overlitigate headlines with modifiers. Be direct, clear and simple.    
3. Use single quotation marks.   
4. Use numbers instead of spelling them out.  
5. Be concise. Omit 👏🏼needless 👏🏼words.👏🏼   
6. Use full names, especially when it’s a household name. And put the names as close to the front of the headline as possible.

**And here are rules about what not to do when writing a headline:**

1. **No commas.** Commas in headline are a print relic and don’t belong on a web story. Avoid writing headlines that separate two items. "New gym is bigger, better."   
2. **Limit the use of two-sentence or grabby web headlines**. Two-sentence headlines are great, but only when the story is really dramatic. (Think of the construction as the first and second acts of a play. You’re setting up the third act, which you reveal in the story itself.) Use those kinds of headlines on surprising profiles and other enterprise stories. Avoid their use elsewhere. Be more direct. The same applies to headlines like “This Maine man did a thing.” These kinds of headlines seem to fall flat with our audience unless the subject is truly interesting, like “This Maine man fought a bear with his bare hands.”   
3. **No jargon.** Audiences should be able to understand what the story is about, even if it’s not something they’re interested in.   
4. **But don’t make them so general they’re boring.** Words like “panel,” “board,” “committee,” “facility” are meaningless.  
5. **No wordplay.** Puns and other clever uses of language are fun to write, but the internet is a literal place. Be clear-eyed and concrete in your use of language.  
6. **Don’t give away the whole story in the headline.** This applies mainly to features stories, not breaking news. Otherwise our audience has no reason to read the story.  
7. **Watch for red flags.** If you see [these words in a headline](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1D6t3iILFdvKj8petsgLKOxjuHFX3awvkliwuKR8mu30/edit), it’s a good sign that you need to rewrite it. 

**Tips for writing a good headline:**

1. Identify the most interesting part of the story. How would someone describe this story to their friend in conversation? What’s the most surprising/informative/entertaining element? Is there a major conflict? The answer to this question \*should\* be found in the lede or nut graf of the story. *If you’re having trouble identifying it, the story likely needs more editing or reporting.*   
2. Similarly, when you’re writing a story you should ask yourself, “So what?” What’s the point of the story? What is the major takeaway? That can also help lead you to a good headline.  
3. Consider the audience. Who do you think this story will appeal to the most? Does it tie into a policy statewide, or does it tap into local nostalgia? Figuring that out can help you decide what information to put in the headline.  
4. Is the story about a government action or policy? If so, focus the headline on what it means for our audience. Make it personal.  
5. Is it the latest in a series? Consider what headlines have worked on similar stories in the past.  
6. Incorporate this interesting info into the Who, What, When, Where, Whys of the story to create a complete headline.  
7. Try writing 5-10 different headlines for the same story. Most of the time, the ideal headline will be a combination of parts from a couple different ones you’ve written.

## Headline red flags

**If you see these words or expressions in a headline, edit to make it more clear and direct.** 

\-- some  
\-- slight  
\-- increase/decrease (replace with better verb: drop, spike, boost, crater)  
\-- flap   
\-- at loggerheads  
\-- incident  
\-- group  
\-- 1-2 punch, perfect storm and other cliches  
\-- potential/potentially  
\-- police investigating (it’s their job, see “police” section of [style guide](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1kr_FlI4nQzjuYZjDKixzYoBecTAiqaCgNLEqnoDvi7Q/edit))  
\-- discussing/talking over  
\-- mulls  
\-- as  
\-- might  
\-- how/why (as the first word in the headline, unless a literal how-to story)  
\-- panel  
\-- board  
\-- committee  
\-- facility  
\-- in part   
\-- alter  
\-- affect  
\-- effect  
\-- impact (never use)  
\-- but   
\-- viral ("viral" is a very high bar. A couple thousand shares on Facebook is not viral.)
